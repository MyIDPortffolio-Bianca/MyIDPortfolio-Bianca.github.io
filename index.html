The Simulation

"How I use AI as a genuine thinking partner — not a content generator — in complex curriculum design."

This artefact documents a live simulation in which I designed a new first-year theology unit from scratch using AI as a collaborative partner. The unit — exploring the intersection of Catholic Social Teaching and Indigenous Australian spirituality — was chosen deliberately for its cultural complexity, ethical stakes, and multi-stakeholder demands. The simulation demonstrates my professional judgement, design process, and critical approach to AI-assisted practice.

What Was Designed

Six-Week Unit: Ways of Knowing

  • WK 1 How Do We Know What We Know? — Introducing epistemology
  • WK 2 Knowing Through Relationship — Community and belonging
  • WK 3 Knowing Through Place — Land, Country, and creation
  • WK 4 Knowing Through Suffering — Justice, healing, and truth
  • WK 5 Knowing Through Practice — Wisdom and professional life
  • WK 6 Knowing Yourself — Reflection and positionality
Moments of Professional Judgement

Where expertise shaped the AI

Correcting the outcomes

AI generated 6 outcomes. I applied curriculum design principles and reduced to 4 — the right number for a 6-week unit.

Challenging the MCQ

AI accepted a proposed MCQ quiz. I flagged its misalignment with the unit's epistemological intent and proposed a scenario-based alternative.

Holding the cultural stakes

I consistently named the ethical weight of Indigenous content — the AI reflected this back but the compass was mine.

Final Unit Learning Outcomes
01

Foundations and Understanding — Ways of knowing, CST principles, Indigenous spiritual foundations

02

Dialogue and Critical Inquiry — Analyse convergence, divergence, and tension across shared human themes

03

Reflection and Cultural Responsibility — Positionality, assumptions, and culturally safe engagement

04

Professional and Ethical Application — Apply both frameworks to disciplinary practice and ethical challenges
